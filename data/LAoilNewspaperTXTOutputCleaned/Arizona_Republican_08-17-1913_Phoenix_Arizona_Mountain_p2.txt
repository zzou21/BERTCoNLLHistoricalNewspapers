The two local baseball teams
known among the fans as the regulars and the natives will clash at

the local diamond this afternoon
at 3:30 sharp. The game means
something to both sides. If the
regulars win they will start climbing
and when Tempe comes over
next Thursday they will take

the worthies into camp. If the Maroons
win it means a breakup in their
usual duck egg average. These two
things alone make a strong fighting
game from the call of the umpire to
the end of the session. The work
of the Indians is always good. They
put up a fighting game from the beginning to the end. It is true that
luck has been breaking against them
in the last few games which they
really deserved to win, but luck is
luck and that's about all.
Talking about luck the regulars
have been having a little of that article of late. They won two games
in a row in good shape and looked
awful good to lead the league when
they went down to Mesa little suspecting that there was a good-twirl
person down there who has a habit
of breaking up exhibitions of the
national pastime. But never mind.
The next game they will win.
There will be a great crowd out to
the game this afternoon. The local
fans support the home team in good
shape now, and they like the way the
little Maroons fight for a game. This
sort of baseball always attracts and
the gate receipts show a big increase
with every game.

Both teams wore out yesterday

afternoon and put in a good hour practicing.
The Maroons used Churchill
field, one of the old time playing
parks of the community, while the
Phoenix team went down to the new
park and worked out in fine style.

SAN FRANCISCO, August 16. Pitcher Ed
Kelly, who has been sold to the
Boston Americans by the Seattle
Northwestern League club to report
in spring, is a Boston youth who
has been living in Spokane. Kelly

joined the Seattle team two weeks
ago. He was bought from the

Walla Walla team, of the Western
Tri-State League, for $300. He has
won three out of five games pitched
for Seattle and last Sunday he made
such a remarkable showing against
the Senators that President Pugdale, of
the Seattle club, feared Kelly would
be traded, and he began negotiations
with Boston to beat the draft. Before
Kelly joined the Walla Walla team
he pitched for the City League of
Spokane.

TEMPLE, August 16. Following a
week of hard practice, fielding and
batting intermixed to the capacity of
every player each evening, Tempe is
in its prime to take on the Mesa,
Highlanders in tomorrow's game at
the local park. Enthusiasm over the
contest is at its height, fans and
players alike are worked up to the
highest pitch and all will play their
respective positions at the park tomorrow and will work as one toward
local victory.
From the camp of the enemy seven
miles up the river comes a similar
cry. And in one respect they have
got it on Tempe, it comes that they
have a new pitcher, recently imported, who has been working out nightly in anticipation of this afternoon's
contest. He goes by the name of
Stovell, is the report, and backs his
reputation with spitters, curves, both
in and out, and straight balls in the
weak spot enough to get the best of

But that is just what the
regulars like, against a real pitcher and
a hard game is just when the
regulars put up such an exhibition as
gives them a right to be contenders

for state champions.
Today's lineup is strictly the best
available and with Spikes, Tempe's
Mar twirler, working on the mound,
big things are being looked for.
Spikes holds the reputation of never
having been replaced in the box in
his life. True, he has been beaten
once or twice by the Mesa Highland
ers, but today he is in his prime
and prepared to work his hardest to
keep Tempe at the head of the Salt
River Valley League.
Behind him, Spikes will have that
invincible fielding team. Nutt, the
Kansas catcher, will be there at his
best. And beside his work at guessing the weak spots he is some hitter.

in the Olympic games. These men
include Sanristo, the Finn, whose javelin throwing was conceded to be the
most marvelous ever witnessed; Hans
Bruun, the German middle distance
runner who followed Sheppard and
Meredith over half of Europe and finally up into Scotland to prove that he
was the best man; W. R. Applegarth,
the English champion sprinter, and
probably the greatest distance runner
of all time; Jean Pouin of France, who
recently ran nearly twelve miles in an
hour, breaking the world's record; and
Taipale, the discus expert, whose best
efforts completely smothered the work
of the Americans and the men of other
nations who competed against him.
One of the best records of the books
today is that of Maxey Pong, former
Columbia University and New York A.
C. short distance runner, whose forty
seven seconds for a straightway quarter of a mile has stood unapproached
since it was made in 1900 at the Out
tenberg racetrack. But at last American
athletics has produced a man in
the person of C. P. Haff of Michigan,
the present champion 440-yard man of
America, both A. A. U. and inter-collegiate,
whose recent grand running at
Boston and Chicago, when he annexed
both titles in convincing fashion, appears to give him a preeminent right to
make a successful essay at the long
standing mark.
Others who are expected to accompany Haff on his long swing down the
quarter stretch of the Empire City
track are Ted Meredith, the Olympic
champion at both 400 metres and 800
metres; Tom Halpin, the Bostonian,
who was the runnerup to Haff in the
Amateur Union championships in Chicago during the early part of the
month; V. V. Wilkie, the Yale lad, who
took all the Crimson quarter-milers
into camp at the dual games between

First of Elimination Trials for
Middleweight Championship.

SAN FRANCISCO, Aug. 16. Promoter Jim Coffroth announced today
that he had signed "Battling Bob"

McAllister and Sailor Petroskey to
meet in a twenty-round contest on
September 12. Articles of agreement
have not been signed but Al McAllister, a brother of Bob, and Coffroth
have agreed to the date, cut in the
gate and other important details.
This will be the first of a series of

elimination contests to determine a
successor to the late Stanley Ketchel. Since the death of Ketchel there has
been no regularly recognized champion.
Dillon, Klaus, Papke, McAllister,
and others claim the title.
