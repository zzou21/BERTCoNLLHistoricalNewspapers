We are requested to announce
that practice for singing at the Congregational church will be held at
the church tomorrow afternoon at
4 o'clock.

The candy pulling that was postponed on account of the bad weather will come off Wednesday evening, Jan. 12, at Mr. John T.
Brawn's, corner of Main and Pico
streets. All are invited.

It is reported that the board of
public works of the city council has
under consideration a proposition
to construct a cable street railway
upon a number of our hill streets,
similar to those in operation in San
Francisco.

Workmen were engaged today in
laying a plank sidewalk along the
Main street front of the courthouse.

C. B. Woodhead, of the firm of
Woodhead and Gay, has just returned from the East. He thinks if
we can command proportionately
good prices with the orange producers of Florida, our fruit will
bring in Chicago from $3 to $5 a
box. Whether we can ship and sell
at these figures will depend entirely
upon the freight rates obtainable.

We have received a sample of the
oil refined by Mr. E. A. Edwards
for illuminating purposes taken out
of the Los Angeles well at Sespe.
It is marked at 110 degrees fire test,
is clear as water and is said to make
a stronger and brighter light than
any of the imported illuminating
fluids now in use here. We shall
try the sample.

Expert is Enthusiastic
(From The Oasis, Sept. 8, 1916)
"You'll strike oil here if you drill
for it," said E. A. Edwards of Mc
Kittrick, Cal., after a visit to the
property of the Easter Oil, Gas and
Mining company, twelve miles south
of Phoenix. "The formation here is
the same as at Tampico, Mexico,
where they struck a 40,000 barrel
well, and the anticlines indicate
that your location of the drilling rig
is the best that could be found on
the entire property. In company
with Secretary-Treasurer Y. L.
Holmes of the company, Mr. Edwards went over practically every
foot of the 2000-acre tract owned
by the oil concern, and declared,
after making the inspection, that
the surface indications of oil in that
vicinity were as good as any he
ever saw in any of the producing
fields.
Although nearly 75 years of age,
Edwards is as keen and alert as
many a man of half his age. He
has been in the oil business for
more than fifty years. He has some
interesting documents in his possession, among them letters from E. L.
Doheny, who, next to John D.
Rockefeller, is probably the richest
oil man in the world, a letter from
the president of the Union Oil Company of California and other letters, all recommending him most
highly as an authority on both oil
refining and oil discovery. He has
a clipping from a Los Angeles newspaper of 1906, of a column, headed,
"Twenty-five Years Ago Today."
Among the interesting paragraphs
contained therein is one telling how
Edwards refined the first oil to be
produced on the coast.

After a vacation of diversified interest
and activity, the clubs of Phoenix are
opening their regular seasons. The
Madison Improvement club and the
Conservation club have already held
their opening meetings and both are in constant use during the hours of 9 in
the morning until 5 in the afternoon.
Since the opening free phonograph records, electric fans, and other small features of
comfort have been installed. The
District Federation of Women's clubs
voted for, toward the furnishing, but
the city has been so liberal in this
matter that this sum will be utilized
for the "little things" that are needed. The contribution of literature
has been generous also and will save
the need of subscription to magazines
and the purchase of books. The
women who have been behind the
movement have not lost interest since
they have secured what has long been
their dream, but will continue toward
its support and maintenance. They
are more enthusiastic than ever in
their belief that women will again turn their
attention to the actual comfort it is
able to give to the country, southside and city
figures prominently in the activity of the women of Phoenix. While practically every club has announced its
opening and routing work will be transacted within the next few weeks. Before the closing dates last spring the
committees had carefully outlined the
program for the ensuing year, never
theless there will be get-together
meetings and discussions and rest room and a playground. Every
committees before the formal opening
of the club season. The Civic League
held its inaugural meeting on Wednesday and will continue its sessions at
the Chamber of Commerce every Wednesday afternoon at 3 o'clock.
The Business Girls' club has not announced its opening, but it is understood the organization is contemplating a gigantic membership rally before
preparing its calendar. The southside
clubs, affiliated with the district and
state federations have outlined interesting work for the next few months
and the outlook everywhere is for a
hard working, brilliant and successful
season.
Through the efforts of public men
and club women, the city is now
maintaining a rest room located on
the city hall grounds at Second and
Jefferson streets. The need of a rest
room had been agitated by the clubs
for years and that it was a real requirement has best been demonstrated by the manner in which the public has taken advantage of it since
its opening the latter part of July.
Mrs. E. H. Abbott, one of its sponsors
and an active member of the Woman's
club, the Musicians' club and the
Arizona Congress of Mothers, is the
matron and reports that the average
number of women and girls who daily
pass from a few minutes to a few
hours there is thirty-five. It is more
than a rest room, for the city authorities have fitted it with every
comfort for the weary shopper. It is a
small house furnished with every
convenience, where the mother can
care for her little one. There are
small iron bedsteads for the tiny
tots and besides the basement bedroom there is a kitchenette with gas
plate and ice chest. On the first
floor the reading room and booth
where parcels may be sent directly
from the shop or checked upon arrival.

EXTS: That we, E. S. CLARK, and
GORDON CLARK, have this day associated ourselves for the purpose of
organizing a corporation under the
laws of the State of Arizona, and for
that purpose do hereby make, adopt
and publish the following Articles of
Incorporation:

ARTICLE I.
The names, residences and post office addresses of the incorporators of
this corporation are as follows:

E. S. CLARK, Prescott, Arizona;
GORDON CLARK, Prescott, Arizona.

ARTICLE II.
The name of this corporation shall
be UNITED WESTERN DEVELOPMENT COMPANY, and the principal
place for the transaction of business
shall be Phoenix, Arizona. The corporation shall have the right to establish
and maintain offices at such other
place or places, either within or without the State of Arizona, as the Board
of Directors of this corporation may
from time to time designate.

ARTICLE III.
The general nature of the business
proposed to be transacted by this corporation is as follows, to-wit:
To carry on a general mining, milling, smelting and reduction business,
and for that purpose to buy, sell, acquire, lease, locate and otherwise deal
in mines, mining claims, mill-sites,
lodes and placers, and all other kinds
of mining property; to work, operate
and develop mining properties, and
mine and reduce the ores therefrom;
to sell and otherwise dispose of any
products derived from the working of
any mining property, or the reduction
of any ores; to buy, build, construct,
own, operate, sell, lease or otherwise
deal in all kinds of mining machinery,
mining equipment, mills and milling
machinery, smelters and smelting machinery, reduction plants and machinery of every kind and character; to
buy, sell, hold, lease, operate and
maintain such railways, tramways,
transportation facilities, telegraph
lines, telephone lines and other methods of transportation and communication, as may be necessary for or incident to the general business of this
corporation; to buy, sell and hold the
stock and securities of this and other
corporations; to acquire, develop, locate, lease and deal in water and water
rights, and to use and control the
same for all lawful purposes; to do a
general merchandise business; to carry
on such other lines of business as may
be incident to the general business of
this corporation, and to do every act
and thing necessary in the carrying on
of a general mining, milling, smelting
and reduction business.

ARTICLE IV.
The capital stock of this corporation
is and shall be one hundred thousand
($100,000.00) dollars, divided into one
hundred thousand shares of the par
value of one dollar each, which stock
shall be issued at such times and upon
such conditions as the Board of Directors may prescribe; and the Board
of Directors are hereby authorized to
issue any or all of the capital stock of
this corporation in consideration of the
payment thereof in cash, or the transfer to this corporation of property,
rights, franchises or interests of any
description, or services rendered or to
be rendered, and the valuation or appraisal of the Board of Directors of
any property, rights, franchises, interests or services rendered or to be rendered, shall be binding and conclusive
upon the stockholders of this corporation and all persons dealing with it.

ARTICLE V.
The affairs of this corporation shall
be managed, administered and controlled by a Board of Directors, consisting of three bona fide stockholders, a majority of whom shall constitute a quorum for the transaction of
business, which directors shall hold
office for one year, and until their
successors shall be elected and qualified. The Board of Directors shall be
elected annually at a regular meeting
of stockholders, which meeting shall be
held on the second Tuesday of June of
each year, at the principal place of
business of the corporation, provided,
that until the first annual meeting
above referred to, E. S. CLARK, H.
K. BARKER, and J. C. NORTON, shall
constitute the Board of Directors, and
a majority of the Board shall have the
power to fill vacancies occurring in
said Board.

ARTICLE VI.
The officers of this corporation shall
be a President, a Vice-President, a
Secretary and a Treasurer, and such
other officers and agents as the Board
of Directors may from time to time
appoint. The officers so mentioned
shall be elected by the Board of Directors from among their own number
and as soon as practicable after their
own election, which officers shall hold
office until the next annual meeting of
the stockholders, and until their successors shall be elected and qualified.
Provided, that until otherwise ordered
by the Board of Directors, the officers
of the Company shall be as follows:
President and General Manager, E.
A. Hitter; Vice President, H. E. Barbee; Secretary and Treasurer, J. C.
Norton.

ARTICLE VII.
The Board of Directors shall have
the power to make such by-laws and
rules for the regulation of the affairs
and business of this corporation as
they shall see fit, and shall have power to alter, repeal and amend the same
at any regular meeting of the Directors.

ARTICLE VIII.
The time for the commencement of
this corporation shall be the 3rd day
of July, 1916, and it shall continue for
twenty-five years from and after that
date.

ARTICLE IX.
These Articles of Incorporation may
be amended by action of the stockholders at any regular meeting or at any
special meeting of stockholders called
for that purpose.

ARTICLE X.
The highest amount of indebtedness
or liability, contingent or otherwise, to
which this corporation may at any
time subject itself shall be fifty thousand ($50,000.00) dollars.

ARTICLE XI.
The private property of the stockholders of this corporation shall be
forever exempt from liability for corporate debts.

ARTICLE XII.
The stock of this corporation shall
when issued, be forever fully paid and
non-assessable.
