OBITUARIES

Joseph Beard
Joseph Beard, 27 years old, son of
Mr. and Mrs. Cyrus Beard, 1121 East
Fillmore street, died Wednesday at the
ranch home at Desert Bend. Besides
the parents, he is survived by a
brother, Clarence Beard, a sister, Mrs.
F. K. Phillips of Phoenix, another
brother who lives in Kansas. Funeral
services will be held this afternoon at
1:30 o'clock at the chapel of Moore
and McLellan. Interment will be at
Greenwood cemetery.

Howard C. Price
Howard C. Price, 47 years old,
died at St. Joseph's hospital at 11 o'clock
Thursday morning. The deceased was
a native of Pennsylvania and a member
of the Jones Optical company firm of
Cleveland, one of the best known optical
firms of the middle west. Together with his wife, Mr. Price came
to Phoenix last November to spend
the winter and was the guest of
Mr. and Mrs. D. F. Harbridge, with whom
Mr. and Mrs. Price attended public
schools in Philadelphia many
years ago. Funeral arrangements will be
announced later.

AMERICANIZATION MEETING
Announcement for a meeting of the
state committee on Americanization is
made by Secretary McBride for Saturday at 1 p.m., in the Arizona club.

MARDI GRAS REHEARSAL Rehearsals for the second presentation
of the children's Mardi Gras are now
in progress. The Royal Party will rehearse this afternoon at 4 o'clock at
241 North First street. Also, again on
Saturday at the American.

CHAS. SCALES FUNERAL Funeral services for Charles Scales will be
held from the Merryman parlors at 2
o'clock this afternoon. Friends of the
deceased may attend the services.

LAST COOKING CLASS The
demonstration lecture course in cookery
which has been conducted by Miss
Winifred Cooley at the high school for
the past six weeks will hold its final
session this afternoon at 4 o'clock.

MADISON IMP. CLUB DANCE The
Madison Improvement club announces
another of their popular dances on
Friday evening, March 6. Good music
is assured and refreshments will be
served. Everyone is cordially invited
to be present.

GOES TO MOTHER'S BEDSIDE
M. Tull, the well known cotton grower,
left last evening for Manhattan, Kansas,
to visit his mother who is seriously
ill. Mrs. Tull is remaining in Phoenix
looking after the extensive cotton
ranch and her husband's flocks of chickens,
until Mr. Tull returns.

TO OBSERVE PURIM Contributions of fruits and cake will be received
at the home of Mrs. S. Rosenbald, 1104
North Third street, until Saturday noon
which the Council of Jewish Women
will distribute to the county farm and
sanitarium in commemoration of the
holiday of Purim, a feast day in the Hebrew calendar.

HUSBAND ASKS DIVORCE Alleging desertion and abandonment,
Chester E. Chalk yesterday instituted
divorce proceedings against Mabel
Chalk in the superior
court of Maricopa County. The couple was married
at Colorado Springs, Colo., in April,
1912, and separated January 1, 1918,
the complaint claims.

SATURDAY RECITAL The Saturday afternoon recital at the School of
Music will be given this week by young
children from the piano primary department in charge of Mrs. Luther
Steward, with violin, dance and expression numbers. These recitals by
the children are very popular and the
public is cordially invited to attend,
the program beginning at 3:30 o'clock.

PATTERSON ESTATE IN COURT
Petition for letters of administration
in the estate of F. J. Patterson, who
died in Phoenix, October 17, 1919, was
filed yesterday by Nerva Patterson,
the widow. The estate, which consists
of real and personal property, is given a
value of $1,500 by the petitioners. Alva
Patterson, an 18-year-old son, and Claudie Ellen Quillin, a 20-year-old
daughter, of Chilhowee, Mo.,
are the heirs, in addition to the widow,
the petition states. Hearing on the
petition will be held in probate court
Monday afternoon, March 15.

WIDOW'S ESTATE IN COURT
Lucinda Cast, a widow, who died in
Phoenix February 29, last, left an
estate of real and personal property
valued at $8,100, according to petition
filed in probate court yesterday by Ernest M.
Cast, a son. The heirs are the petitioner,
and two daughters, Edna M.
Cast and Alice Cast, the petition states.
Hearing on the petition will be held
Monday afternoon, March 15, at 3:30
o'clock.

invited to be present.

HORNBERGER IS DELEGATE W.
C. Hornberger, superintendent of the
Pacific Gas and Electric company, has
been chosen by the board of directors
of the Phoenix Chamber of Commerce
to represent that organization at the
Parent-Teachers association and Congress of Mothers dinner and meeting
at the Woman's club next Monday.

ONLY TWO FALSE ALARMS
Children and practical jokers were
comparatively inactive during the
month of February according to the
report of the fire department. Only
two false alarms were turned in during
the month out of a total of 15
calls. Although there were 13 fires
during the month of February, the
total fire loss was only $2,290.

WOMAN'S TRADE UNION
LEAGUE All members of the Women's Trade Union League are requested to attend a meeting of the
league to be held at the Trades Union
hall, Friday afternoon, March 5, at
2:30 o'clock.

JURY FAILS TO AGREE Failing
to agree after deliberations extending
over a period of 20 hours, the jury
which heard the testimony in the forcible entry and detainer action of E. E.
Oldaker, et al. against R. H. Brumback,
et al, was dismissed by Judge Frank
Lyman in division two of the superior
court at 1:30 o'clock yesterday afternoon. Earlier in the day the jurymen
had reported that they were unable to
agree but were sent back to their
chambers for further deliberations.
The action was set for trial a second
time March 3.

GRANTS WIFE DIVORCE Mildred
Lewenthal yesterday was granted a
divorce decree from Henry Allen Le-
wenthal by Judge Frank Lyman in
the superior court. The decree provides that the mother shall have possession of the only minor child and
that the father shall contribute $30 a
month towards its support.

GETS SIX MONTHS' SENTENCE
A. Houle of Chandler, arrested last
week on a charge of obtaining labor
under false pretense, was given a six
months' suspended sentence by Judge
Maniora in the criminal division
of the superior court yesterday. The
suspended sentence provides, however,
that Houle must pay the men formerly
in his employ salaries due them and
ranging from $2.40 to $54.80.

THIS IS TAX TIME Maricopa
county's taxpayers will do well today
to wear their old clothes, hide their
twins-sixes in the brush, and hang out
for sale signs, for the tax-making authorities are going forth to look them
over with a view of deciding how much
to tax them for this year's taxes. The
authorities include the members of the
state tax commission, the county supervisors, and Deputy Assessor H. G.
Powers. Assessor Cummins is reported too ill with the flu to go along on
the joyful trip.

COCONINO ASSESSOR HERE J.
D. Dunn, assessor of Coconino county,
was in the city yesterday to confer
with the state tax commissioner.

WILL IS FILED The will of the
late Michael Edward Murphy, in
which the estate is bequeathed to the
wife, Margaret Murphy, was filed for
probate yesterday. Mr. Murphy died
in Phoenix March 3, last. The estate
consists of $2,000 worth of United
States Liberty bonds, bills and accounts receivable amounting to $2,500,
some oil stock of doubtful value and
also community property. The will,
which names the widow as sole executrix of the estate, bore the date of
October 15, 1910.

SEARCHES FOR UNCLE Bob Mc
Murray, described as a cattleman who
has worked most of his life for
others in Phoenix district and at one
time was some kind of an officer, is
wanted by his nephew, John E. Deckson, box 481, Tucson, according to a
letter signed by Deckson, which was
received by the Phoenix chamber of
commerce yesterday. McMurray is
from Ft. Worth, Tex. Deckson says
any information will be appreciated by
him.

THEORY THAT LEAP
YEAR IS UNLUCKY
In all probability the theory that
leap year is unlucky was voiced by
the unhappy man who first was called
upon to answer a woman's leap year
proposal that he bestow upon her his
"hand and fortune or a braw silk gown."

The Japanese believe that the 210th
and the 220th days of each leap year
are particularly unlucky, while the old
English traditions maintain that children born in leap year will be sickly
and difficult to raise, and that crops
will not prosper, peas and beans
growing on the wrong side of the pod."
