Choose your druggist carefully.

AN EBONY CANE. Mr. T. K. Elvey
has a souvenir of the Philippines that
he prizes very highly for two reasons.
First, because it was brought back to
him by his son, who served with the
Thirty-fourth regiment, and, secondly,
because it is about the nicest thing in
the cane line that anybody ever had. It
is made of ebony and is most exquisitely
carved. Both the design and the ex
ecution of the work are marvelous.

A YOUTHFUL TRAVELER. Rus
sell Mullin, a young gentleman from
Los Angeles, about six or seven years
old, arrived here yesterday morning on
the S. F., P. & P. train on a visit to his
grandmother, Mrs. E. R. Bishop,
who lives on North Fourth avenue. He
made the journey alone and experienced
no difficulty. In fact he was quite a
business-like sort of a boy, and when he
got here he hired a hack, gave his
grandmother's address, and traveled in
as much style as though he were fifty
years old and owned a railroad. He
probably will some day.

MORE ABOUT OATS. This part of
Arizona has not made much of a repu
tation as an oat growing country,
that is when the oats are desired for
grain. But when it comes to
raising oat hay, there is no place under
the sun where water and soil will do
more business with oats, properly
planted, than right here in this valley.
They can be raised for grain, too,
as far as that is concerned, but for
that purpose a poorer soil is desirable,
so the grain will fill out and the nour
ishment will not all go to stalk. But
oat hay is where the Salt River valley
shines. Mention was made a day or
two ago of a sample of oats brought
into the board of trade. Yesterday Mr.
Austin Starkweather, who is farming
a quarter-section belonging to T. K.
Elvey, and which for private reasons
he has been trying to dispose of, yes
terday brought him in a bunch of oats
that was really surprising. The straw
measured seven feet and one inch in
length, and off a little distance looks
more like bamboo poles than like oat
straw. Still it is tender and nutritious
and makes just as good hay as the
little weakly straw that grows in some
countries where oat hay is made a
specialty.

PRESS ASSOCIATION MEETING.
The members of the Arizona Press
association are respectfully invited to
attend a called meeting of the associa
tion at Phoenix, Arizona, on May 6,
1901. The objects of the meeting are
to assist in greeting and making Presi
dent McKinley welcome to Arizona on
his western visit and for the further
purpose of arranging to entertain the
various newspaper men who are accom
panying the president on his Pacific
coast tour while he is this city on May 7.
It is earnestly hoped that every mem
ber of the association will be present.
M. H. McCORD, President.
E. S. PERKINS, Secretary.
