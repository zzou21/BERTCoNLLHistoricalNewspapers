J. K. Breitwieser, representing the Rand Oil Company, which owns or holds interests in extensive tracts near Tempe, was in town yesterday. Mr. Breitwieser said that his company was ready to commence boring near Tempe if oil should be struck in the neighborhood. The Arizona Standard Company, which has land near his place, upon which it is now drilling, is affiliated with the Rand Oil Company and should oil be found near Tempe by the former company, or if the indications become very favorable, they will take up the work at once with improved machinery.
The Alpha Literary Society, at the Normal School, will celebrate Saturday evening the most auspicious occasion in the history of the organization. The society has been donated a handsome new banner and to celebrate this event in proper style the members of the society will carry out a varied program embracing musical and literary features of no mean merit. The public is cordially invited to be present. On March 29 Mesa is to have one of the greatest treats the quiet little town has enjoyed for some time. The great Uneeda Club, who staged the minstrels so successfully Friday night in Tempe, have engaged the opera house in Mesa for the last Friday in March. The fame of the minstrels is now widespread and it is expected that the house will be crowded. On April 11 or thereabouts the same club will give a second exhibition in Tempe. It is rumored that the ladies will be invited to participate in the show. With a program as good as the last and enacted by young ladies instead of witty, but more or less ugly boys, the success of the show would be unparalleled.

The two lodges will organize a similar lodge in Phoenix tonight, where Mrs. Alden says they will have fifty members.

One of the largest congregations that has gathered in Mesa for a long time assembled Sunday at 2 p.m. at the funeral of Henry C. Rogers, who died Saturday morning at his home in Lehi. The large tabernacle was handsomely decorated with palms and evergreens, and the pulpit was draped with cream-colored cloth and green twigs. The building was crowded to its fullest and there were many who could not gain admittance. Among the audience were people from all over the valley, who came to show their feelings of sympathy for the bereaved family and respect for the departed one. Long before the time for the funeral, the natives who had learned to love "Brother Rogers" began to arrive and the street north of the building was lined with sad-faced Pimas, Maricopas and Papagos.
There were on the stand, besides the bishoprics of the various wards of the stake and high priests and elders, President Brigham Young of the quorum of apostles, President C. R. Hakes, and W. J. LeParon of the stake presidency. President C. R. Hakes presided at the meeting. The choir sang "You Who Weep, Weep Not Alone." Prayer was offered by Bishop Samuel Openshaw of Nephi ward, followed by the choir in "Rock of Ages, Cleft for Me."
Elder F. T. Pomeroy was the first speaker. He spoke of the long and useful life the deceased had lived, and how faithfully he had fulfilled the trusts given to him. "He was a Godly man, and the guiding star of his life was that which characterized the life of his Master. 'Father, Thy will be done and not mine.' He had the love of all the people with whom he was acquainted in every walk of life, whether on the plains or deserts, in the halls of the legislature in which he faithfully served his country one session, in his temporal associations, his ecclesiastical, or in the family circle, he was the same honest, steadfast, cheerful, loving man of God. He was a man with a purpose in life, and no obstacle would swerve him from the path of duty. He was an exceedingly pious man, humble and full of faith in his God and his purposes, and if the history of his life should be written, in it would be found visions, dreams, answers to prayer, and the manifestations of Providence in his behalf, the like of which has been enjoyed by but few men that have lived upon earth. He was humble and modest and looked upon all mankind as his brethren and sisters. Even the Indians had been blessed by his counsels, and hundreds had been brought into the folds of Christ through his ministrations, and as a unit, they were now in mourning over his departure. We rejoice in his noble life and mourn his departure."
Elder C. K. Hakes, Jr., said that in the life of Brother Henry C. Rogers is found an example for all mankind to follow. Throughout his life he had left a trail of blessings behind him, and we mourn not for him, for he is all right, but we mourn because we will miss his presence, his counsel and his influence in life. His life was one grand testimony to the Gospel and of the Master whom he served, and he has gone to his reward. May we reach ours as worthily.
President Brigham Young was the next speaker, and he referred to the life of Brother Henry C. Rogers in eloquent terms. He declared him to have been a "man of God, and gone to what I have been laboring for many years to obtain. It was but Tuesday last that I had a long visit with Brother Henry, and when I meet him beyond the veil, I expect to greet him as I did at that time. I will throw my arms around him. Will I know him? Yes. I knew him before we came to this earth.

A daughter of Valenzuela, who has been attending the Indian school at Phoenix, and is quite well educated and talented, sang "Nearer My God to Thee," in clear melodious tones, that delighted as well as saddened the audience by the pathos and deep feeling that was manifest, which seemed a message from her people to one they loved.
Valenzuela, a native missionary companion of Brother Rogers, then spoke through an interpreter, Bishop G. M. Tiffany, and said in part that he had labored with Brother Rogers for eighteen years, had traveled the deserts by day and night with him, and knew him to be a good man. He has gone to his rest, and will receive his salvation in the presence of the Lord. I have just returned from a visit to the natives in the south and brought many expressions of love from them to him, but I found him gone and it is all right. We all loved him and mourn at his departure.
President C. R. Hakes then spoke for a short time, and referred feelingly to the pleasant labors he had enjoyed with the deceased during their association in the presidency of the Maricopa stake. "He was one of the most humble and modest men I ever knew. He was fearless in expressing his opinions in our councils, but whenever a decision was rendered, no matter where he stood on the proposition, he would lend every energy to carry out the measure. He was broad-minded and charitable, had the love of God in his heart, and has gone where I desire to go when my time comes, and where we should all go, for Brother Henry has "gone to his reward," as there is a crown in heaven. I pray God to comfort his widow, and He will comfort her, as well as her children. And I say to them, as Brother Brigham said, 'Pick up the reins where he let go of them and continue in the path he was treading and all will be well with you.' " He related the calling of Henry C. Rogers to go south by President Brigham Young early in the 70s. He had lived for twenty-five years in Provo, all his family and relatives were there, and when he stepped into President Young's office and was asked if he didn't want to "go south into Arizona" to establish a colony, he replied, "If you ask me if I want to go, I tell you plainly, no. But if you ask me to go, I say, yes, when shall I start." And here he came and has been here for forty years, and would not be moved from his place unless the same authority said so. He has gone to his rest. God help us to emulate his example and to go where he is, at the end.
After the choir sang, the congregation was dismissed by Hyrum S. Phelps, and the congregation was permitted to view the remains. It was truly pathetic to see the natives file past and view the face of him whom they had loved in life and mourned in death. And they were honest tears that they shed. After viewing the remains they filed past the wife and family of the deceased and shook them silently by the hands, with tears in their eyes, mute witnesses to the love they had for the deceased and their sympathy for the bereaved ones.
There were over eighty carriages following the hearse to the cemetery.
ARTICLES OF INCORPORATION
of the
WATOSA COMPANY.
Be it known, that the undersigned, having voluntarily associated themselves together with the intention of forming a corporation under the laws of the Territory of Arizona, have adopted and hereby do adopt the following articles of incorporation:
ARTICLE I.
The name of the corporation shall be The Watosa Company.
The general nature of its business shall be to buy and otherwise acquire, manufacture and sell patent, proprietary and other medicines of all kinds; also flavoring extracts, ointments, salves, liniments, lotions, spices and condiments; and to do anything that is convenient in connection therewith, properly incident thereto, and necessarily connected with the same and any part thereof; also, to borrow money and execute and deliver notes and mortgages to secure the same, and to acquire, purchase, hold and sell, and otherwise deal in real and personal property, including the shares of stock of this or other like corporations; and generally to do and perform any and all business and acts in which individuals may lawfully associate themselves to perform. The principal place of transacting the business of said corporation shall be Phoenix, county of Maricopa, Territory of Arizona, with a branch office in the city of Winona, county of Winona and state of Minnesota.
ARTICLE II.
This corporation shall commence on the first day of March, 1902, and shall continue for a period of twenty-five years.
ARTICLE III.
The amount of the capital stock of said corporation shall be $15,000.00, divided into 1,500 shares of the face value of $10.00 each, and shall be issued and paid for as ordered by the board of directors; said board of directors may issue preferred stock in said corporation, but only on such terms and under such conditions as may be prescribed by the by-laws; and the highest amount of indebtedness and liability to which said corporation shall at any time be subject, including preferred stock, issued and outstanding, shall not exceed the sum of $10,000.00.
ARTICLE IV.
The names and places of residence of the persons forming this corporation are as follows: Joseph Shackell, Ernest H. Smith, T. S. Estabrook, George T. Simpson, Paul E. Baumgartner, Henry J. Willis, O. T. Caswell, James K. Simpson and Will Hayes Laird, all of the city of Winona, Minnesota.
ARTICLE V.
The government of this corporation and the management of its affairs shall be vested in a board of five directors, who shall be stockholders; said directors shall be elected at the annual meeting of the stockholders, which shall be held on the first Monday in March of each year, at such hour and place as may be prescribed by the by-laws, and such directors shall continue in office for one year, and until their successors shall have been elected and qualified. The meetings of the stockholders and of the board of directors may be held without the Territory of Arizona and at the said city of Winona, Minnesota. The first board of directors shall consist of Joseph Shackell, T. S. Estabrook, James K. Simpson, Henry J. Willis and O. T. Caswell, who shall hold their office until the annual meeting of the stockholders in the year 1903, and until their successors are elected and qualified. The officers of said corporation shall be a president, a vice-president, a secretary and a treasurer, and they shall be annually elected by the directors at their first meeting after their own election. One person may be chosen to hold any two of the said offices at the same time, except the office of president and vice-president. Until their successors are elected and qualified, Joseph Shackell shall be president and treasurer, James K. Simpson shall be vice-president and treasurer, and T. S. Estabrook shall be secretary. Any vacancy in the board of directors may be filled by the board at any regular or special meeting. Special meetings of the board may be held at any time and place, and under such notice as may be prescribed by the by-laws. All elections must be by ballot, and every stockholder shall have the right to vote in person or by proxy, the number of shares standing in his name upon the books of the company, for as many persons as there are directors to be elected, or to cumulate said shares and give one candidate as many votes as the number of directors multiplied by the number of shares shall equal, or distribute them on the same principle, or among as many candidates as he shall see fit.
ARTICLE VI.
The private property of the incorporators and of the stockholders in said corporation is exempt from the payment of the corporate debts, and there shall be no individual liability to stockholders and directors for their acts as such.
ARTICLE VII.
The shares of stock of this corporation shall be forever non-assessable.
